## c) ACCOUNTING PRINCIPLES AND ASSUMPTIONS

### Fundamental Accounting Principles

#### 1. Going Concern Principle
The company prepares financial statements on the assumption that it will continue to operate in the foreseeable future. There are no plans or necessity to liquidate or curtail operations.

#### 2. Accrual Basis of Accounting
All transactions are recorded on an accrual basis, meaning:
- Revenues are recognized when earned, regardless of when cash is received
- Expenses are recognized when incurred, regardless of when cash is paid

#### 3. Consistency Principle
Accounting methods and policies are applied consistently from period to period. Any changes in accounting policies must be disclosed and justified.

#### 4. Materiality Principle
Transactions and events are recorded if they are material to the financial statements. Materiality is assessed based on both quantitative and qualitative factors.

#### 5. Prudence (Conservatism) Principle
When there is uncertainty, assets and revenues should not be overstated, and liabilities and expenses should not be understated.

#### 6. Historical Cost Principle
Assets are initially recorded at their historical cost (acquisition cost). Subsequent revaluation is only permitted under specific circumstances as per accounting standards.

#### 7. Matching Principle
Expenses are matched with the revenues they help generate in the same accounting period.

#### 8. Full Disclosure Principle
All material information that could affect users' understanding of the financial statements must be disclosed.

### Accounting Assumptions

#### 1. Economic Entity Assumption
The company is treated as a separate economic entity, distinct from its owners and other entities.

#### 2. Monetary Unit Assumption
Financial statements are expressed in terms of a stable monetary unit ([Currency]). Transactions in foreign currencies are converted using appropriate exchange rates.

#### 3. Time Period Assumption
The company's operations can be divided into specific time periods (months, quarters, years) for reporting purposes.

#### 4. Cost-Benefit Assumption
The benefits of providing accounting information should exceed the costs of providing it.

#### Liability Recognition Policy
